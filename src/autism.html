Invisible Autism

February 2022

This is a more complete version of a comment I left on HN. I recommend reading the subject article by C.L. Lynch, "Autism is a spectrum" doesn't mean what you think.

I have autism. If you know me, that might surprise you. I appear neurotypical. I have a career, a marriage, and a social life. I like playing video games and fighting climate change. I don't need support from others on a daily basis.

And yet my shrink, a 30-year clinical psychologist and behavioral health department director, wrote it right there on my medical chart:

Autism spectrum disorder

Behind the scenes, there's a "me" hardly anyone sees. It's the me you can paralyze with a text message, whose entire day is ruined if I brush my teeth after showering instead of before, and whose arm feels on fire if you lightly draw little circles on it with your finger. It's the me that was mute for nine years growing up.

I'm not an outlier. In fact, 2% of the world has autism [1]. Is that a surprise to you? Do you even believe me?

The Infamous Spectrum

We've all heard "autism is a spectrum". But that word, "spectrum", has had its original meaning lost to time. A spectrum is not a scale. This is a spectrum:

 

There is no "more" or "less"; no "high" or "low". There is only "different".

But clearly, something is going on here. Every type of neurodivergence has its shitty parts about "coming out", but autism seems to have a uniquely high rate of responses along the lines of "I don't believe you".

People believe autism looks like X so strongly that if they don't see X, they won't even believe a professional diagnosis. Why? What is X?

The Canary Symptom

Autism is a grab bag of symptoms, and not all people with autism are dealt the same hand. One of the possible symptoms is the following:

Deficits in developing, maintaining, and understand [sic] relationships, ranging, for example, from difficulties adjusting behavior to suit various social contexts; to difficulties in sharing imaginative play or in making friends; to absence of interest in peers.

DSM-5

Let's let that stew for a moment and take a brief detour.

A Personal Example

One symptom I got dealt is a hearing hypersensitivity. As cool as it sounds, this is no superpower. Instead, my body's defense systems will sometimes malfunction and incorrectly believe everything is far too loud.

During one of these bouts, all the neurological safety mechanisms of an air horn being blown point-blank into my ear will activate in response to things as quiet as a book page turning: startle, flinching, irritability, inability to think, inability to carry on a conversation. Just you know, the all-around feeling of "losing it".

Imagine going to the grocery store and at any given moment dozens of people are point-blank blowing air horns into your ears. Maybe you'll understand me then, when I say that as a kid I would sometimes collapse to the floor and roll around screaming with my hands against my ears, begging to go home.

But! I'm one of the lucky ones. I didn't get dealt the "difficulties adjusting behavior to suit various social contexts" symptom. So when other people in the grocery store look at me like I'm losing it (reminder: I am) I can log that detail away for next time, and try a little harder to keep it in. I've gone through several stages over 30+ years:

  1. Rolling on the floor screaming
  2. Covering my ears with my palms and closing my eyes as hard as I could
  3. Sitting down in the aisle quietly and trying to tune everything out
  4. Subtly putting my fingers in my ears and screaming interally
  5. Inventing excuses to stay home
  6. (all grown up) Just leaving when it happens

Although my actions are more socially acceptable now, the sensation is the same. Inside, I'm still that little kid rolling on the floor screaming, trying to get away. The only difference is that I have learned to fake it.

Play the Hand You're Dealt

Unfortunately, people dealt the "difficulties adjusting behavior to suit various social contexts" card get stuck on the rolling-on-the-floor-screaming phase. This, perhaps, is what the world thinks autism "looks like".

People who can't act the pain away often need more support because our world is a social world. But they are not any more "low-functioning" than I am "high-functioning". Those are descriptions of how observers experience our autism, not how we do. This is why these terms have fallen out of use since the DSM-5.

Masking

In the autism community we call the ability to fake not having autism masking. Those of us who can do it, do it ~every day.

Masking a hearing hypersensitivity is just one example. Autism can come with any sensory hypersensitivity, and any of several other symptoms. For many, you can find some great masking advice in online communities.

Other symptoms people may mask are "stimming" (e.g. rocking or hand flapping; a positive behavior), lack of facial expressions, inability to cope with ambiguity or changing plans, and more.

Masking comes with its own set of issues [2], particularly that it's enourmously exhausting; it's basically repurposing active brainpower to fill the shoes of missing social common sense. For me personally, this leaves me with less brainpower left to think through complex problems while in social situations—I literally am dumber when I am speaking.

Most people with autism agree that the best part of the day is that first moment alone when you can finally "unmask". That said, for all its downsides masking is a great tool and being able to use it is a blessing.

A Different Way to Think About Autism

2% of the world has autism. I didn't find out I did until I was 30 years old, and it explained so much. Maybe if I hadn't been so incentivized to hide it, I would have found out sooner.

Please encourage yourself and others to feel comfortable talking with family, friends, and doctors about ways you might feel different from others. It's a hard thing, to know if your brain is doing something different from other people's brains.

If someone talks to you about it, be open to the idea that they might be onto something, instead of dismissing it as a character weakness to be overcome like so many people in my life did.

In the end, the best thing I got out of it was just knowing what was wrong.
