Gut-driven development

July 26, 2019

I don't like how much stress is placed on being data-driven.

Data is nice. It helps you make decisions. But I see companies experimenting, A/B testing, whittling down every decision into a data-driven perfection sent from the heavens. Every little thing gets stalled at the "Can we show this in data?" phase.

And sure, data lets you know if users are making it through your funnel. But do you know anything deeper than that? Do you know if those users are being charmed, or feeling butterflies?

A case study

Everyone knows you can't track everything you need to make good decisions. But let's walk through an example: Assassin's Creed.

The first Assassin's Creed game was a risky move for a game developer to make, and it paid off! It had shortcomings, but all risks do. It had interesting tech and it was a fresh genre.

I remember being flabbergasted at the crowdplay in the Assassin's Creed E3 demo, like pushing crowdmembers out of the way or hiding in a group of them to avoid guards. youtube

The Assassin's Creed II trilogy resolved some of those shortcomings and took some more risks. Great! That's what a sequel to a risky game should do (see Portal 2).

By Assassin's Creed III, it was clear that Ubisoft had taken this series under a different wing—the "Oh, it's successful?" wing, also known as the cow-milking wing. Things didn't change much in Assassin's Creed games after that. They had invented the perfect cow, and now they could sell milk.

The games started looking more or less like yearly sports games. Ubisoft announced proudly that it had spun up a second Assassin's Creed team, so they could trade off releasing every year. Wow! What a great idea.

Division of labor between Assassin's Creed teams. kotaku

I don't know about you, but it was around this time that I started finding myself disliking Assassin's Creed games, but still playing them—an interesting phenomenon to live through. They were meticulously crafted time sinks. Just fun enough to keep you playing, and not one iota more.

This is the fast food industry of gaming. Cold, calculating, efficient—and effective! Assassin's Creed games are wildly successful because of this strategy. But it overlooks one thing.

Loyalty

People catch on. They realize they're only being sated, not satisfied. It might take one game, or two, or ten, but eventually people stop bothering to keep buying the games.

The opposite of this strategy is Nintendo. Nintendo doesn't give a shit. They love making amazing games, data be damned.

Because you know what? Nintendo makes amazing and unique games. They take risks, they do weird stuff, and it sometimes flops. But they never milk the cow too long before moving on. The Wii U was a flop. Jumping around your living room to play Wii was a flop. But they weren't mistakes. The Wii U was v0.5 of the Switch. Jumping around your living room turned into gyroscopic aiming.

Nintendo's vision for families bonding and exercising over the Wii never really took off past the experimental honeymoon phase. And that's okay. nintendo life

For every mistake, Nintendo gets something right. The control stick. The 3D platformer. The twin-stick first-person shooter. The home video game system. The handheld console. The open-world adventure game. The metroidvania. Where would the video game industry be if Nintendo's strategy was to milk the cow?

Ubisoft and Nintendo are both successful companies by many measures, so it's hard to point at them and say "I like this one better, so the other one must be flawed". Any company as successful as Ubisoft must have done many things right, and far be it from me to tell them their philosophy is wrong.

But I'm a small business owner, so it's important for me to make sure my business's philosophy is in tune with my ideals.

I want to be like Nintendo. And I don't know for sure to what extent they use data, but anyone who's interacted with their software knows it's probably at least an order of magnitude less than your classic data-driven company.

Gut-driven development

I'll be honest, I chose Nintendo for this case study because I'm a Nintendo superfan, and if there's one place I'll let myself be biased it's on my own website explaining my own philosophy.

I always get in line about 18 hours before any Nintendo console launches. It's super chill, people passing cocoa, playing guitar, and being thankful Nintendo launches don't show up in murder investigations, news stories about being trampled to death, and eBay listings at a 1,000% markup like the PlayStation and Xbox launches do.

The chillest crowd. nintendo world report

It's a small town's worth of people, but it feels like a community of chillfolk. But for the Wii U, something was different. No one else showed up. I had the cops called on my by (presumably) a store employee leaving for the night, thinking I was homeless. By the time the doors opened, only nine people had lined up.

I was thinking Where is everybody? Did I get the date right?.

Of course, spoiler alert, everyone was home because the Wii U sucked. It was poorly marketed, poorly explained ("Wait, so it's an add-on for my Wii?") and had no hype launch games. The only three games worth buying the console over were Splatoon, Wind Waker HD, and Twilight Princess HD, none of which were launch games, and two of which people had already played.

So Nintendo failed me. I'd done what superfans do: I gave my heart, my time, and my money, and it was all a big let-down.

This is just what comes out of their brains. You can't make it by looking at data.

Loyalty bars aren't exposed in data, at least not until a literal lifetime of good data collection goes by. But everyone has one.

Every time a bad decision is made, it might reflect nicely in the data regardless of user opinion. If you make it hard for users to cancel their subscriptions, will fewer users cancel their subscriptions? Probably! Does that mean you should do it?

2. It's inefficient

If you're unconvinced, here's a data-driven way you can evaluate data-driven development. Try this at home.

Being data-driven is hard. The instrumentation, the extra tests, the extra specs, the coordination involved with them, agreeing what should be instrumented and when, periodically making sure everything you're instrumenting is consistent and well-formatted, dealing with typos in events and properties, and doing it for every feature and maintaining it for every previously-built feature certainly takes its toll.

Go ahead and make the 80/20 trade-off—put your Google Analytics snippet in your pages or whatever. But instrumenting every single tiny little thing is just not worth it.

The landscape that results from a data-driven setup is strictly better if you know how and when to use it, but if you could ditch all that extra framework, scaffolding, and manpower in exchange for a 10x or 100x velocity on gut-driven features... wouldn't you?

What if your team while data-driven team can churn out a feature a month with a 75% hit rate, but while gut-driven team can churn out 10 or 100 features a month with a 25% hit rate? Do you know what your team's velocity could be without this extra framework around their work?

Don't know the answer? Run an experiment.

Conclusion

Loyalty bars defy data. They're not special snowflakes, but they are really hard to measure, and they're orders of magnitude more important than the things that are easy to measure. But if you one day discovered users just wanted to be charmed and feel butterflies... wouldn't you charm them and build butterflies?
